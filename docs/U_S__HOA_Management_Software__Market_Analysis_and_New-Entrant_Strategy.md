# U.S. HOA management software: Market analysis and new-entrant strategy

**The HOA management software market is a $1.5B–$2.5B opportunity growing at 9–12% CAGR — not the 15% sometimes claimed — with the most underpenetrated segment being the ~110,000 self-managed communities under 100 units where 75% still rely on spreadsheets.** This report synthesizes data from the Community Associations Institute, Foundation for Community Association Research, multiple market research firms, SaaS benchmarking studies, and direct competitor pricing analysis to deliver a complete market sizing, segment analysis, pricing strategy, unit economics framework, and go-to-market plan for a new entrant.

---

## The HOA landscape: 370,000 associations and growing

The United States has approximately **365,000–370,000 community associations** as of 2024–2025, encompassing 28.8 million housing units and 77–80 million residents — roughly one-third of all U.S. housing. The Foundation for Community Association Research (FCAR) projects this will reach **~377,000 associations by 2026**, with 3,000–5,000 new communities forming annually. Critically, **82.4% of new homes sold in 2023 were in HOA communities**, ensuring sustained structural growth.

The size distribution skews heavily toward small communities. Average community size is **~77 units**, and the best available survey data (2024 Buildium/CAI Industry Report, n=536 board members) shows:

| Community size | Share of all HOAs | Estimated count |
|---|---|---|
| **Under 100 units** | ~60% | ~222,000 |
| **100–400 units** | ~30% | ~111,000 |
| **400–1,000 units** | ~5–7% | ~18,500–26,000 |
| **1,000+ units** | ~2–3% | ~7,400–11,100 |

Three types compose the market: planned communities/HOAs (58–63%), condominium associations (35–40%), and housing cooperatives (2–4%). Top states by volume are California (~50,700), Florida (~49,800), Texas (~22,300), and Illinois (~19,550). Collectively, these associations manage **$108.8 billion in annual assessments** — a massive financial operations base that drives demand for management software.

## Self-managed HOAs are the largest underserved segment

The CAI Statistical Review reports that **30–40% of community associations are self-managed**, meaning they operate with volunteer board members and no professional management company. The 2024 Buildium/CAI survey puts the figure at **28%**, down from 33% the prior year, suggesting a gradual trend toward professional management. Using the midpoint estimate of ~30%, approximately **110,000–130,000 HOAs are self-managed**.

The self-managed/professional split correlates strongly with community size:

- **Under 50 units**: Predominantly self-managed. Professional management at $20–$40/unit/month would consume 25–75% of a small community's budget, making it economically irrational.
- **50–200 units**: Mixed. Communities in this range often debate whether to hire management or continue self-managing.
- **200+ units**: Overwhelmingly professionally managed, with budgets large enough to justify $10–$30/unit/month management fees.

Self-managed HOAs face acute pain points that software can address: **manual dues collection** (creating awkward neighbor-to-neighbor payment chasing), **volunteer burnout** (administrative tasks approaching part-time workloads), **communication gaps** (scattered email chains, paper notices), **document management chaos** (CC&Rs, minutes, and financials stored on personal devices), and **compliance risk** (volunteers lacking knowledge of state-specific HOA laws). When board members turn over — which happens frequently — institutional knowledge evaporates.

The critical insight: **75% of all community associations still rely on spreadsheets, paper files, or no formal tools at all**. Only about 25% use dedicated management software. Among self-managed communities, adoption is likely **15–25%**, representing a vast greenfield opportunity. Meanwhile, **72% of residents say they prefer using technology** for HOA communication — demand exists, but supply hasn't reached most small communities.

## Market size reality check: $2B growing at 10%, not $6B at 15%

The claim that the HOA management software market is growing from $2B to $6B by 2033 at 15% CAGR **does not hold up to scrutiny**. Cross-referencing 20+ market research reports reveals the $6B/$15% figures originate from a single lesser-known firm (Data Insights Market) that published internally inconsistent reports — one citing $2B and another citing $750M for approximately the same market in the same year.

The actual picture, synthesized from credible sources:

| Source | Current size | Forecast | CAGR | Scope |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Verified Market Research | $1.78B (2024) | $4.68B (2032) | 10.5% | HOA-specific |
| Business Research Insights | $1.8B (2024) | $5.5B (2033) | 13.2% | HOA-specific |
| Wise Guy Reports | $2.61B (2024) | $5.2B (2032) | 9.0% | HOA-specific |
| Grand View Research | $3.61B (2025) | $5.89B (2033) | 6.4% | Broader property mgmt |
| Mordor Intelligence | $6.53B (2026) | $9.93B (2031) | 8.7% | Broader property mgmt |
| Data Horizzon Research | $2.8B (2024) | $7.2B (2033) | 11.1% | Community assn. mgmt |

**Defensible consensus for the HOA-specific software market: ~$1.5B–$2.5B today, growing to $3B–$5B by 2032–2033 at 9–12% CAGR.** The broader property management software market (which includes HOA as a subsegment alongside multifamily, commercial, and student housing) is $3.5B–$6.5B today. Market segmentation is critical — reports labeled "HOA software" at $9B+ are clearly misclassified and include the entire property management ecosystem.

Cloud-based deployment dominates at **55–63% market share** and is the fastest-growing segment. North America represents **36–46% of the global market**. Growth drivers include increasing HOA formation rates, rising digital expectations from homeowners, payment processing digitization, and regulatory complexity pushing communities toward structured tools.

## Competitor pricing reveals clear strategic whitespace

Analysis of 15+ competitors reveals three distinct pricing architectures, each aligned to a different market segment:

**Tiered flat-rate (targeting self-managed HOAs):**
- **PayHOA**: $49/mo (≤50 units) → $99/mo (51–150) → $199/mo (151–300) → $249/mo (301–500). All features included at every tier. The clearest pricing in the market.
- **RunHOA**: $399/year flat, unlimited units. Cheapest option overall.
- **HOA Start**: $39/mo starting price.
- **EasyHOA**: $49/mo (1–25 units), all-inclusive.

**Per-unit/month (targeting management companies):**
- **AppFolio**: $0.80–$5.00/unit/month, $280 minimum. 50-unit minimum portfolio.
- **SmartWebs**: From $0.40/door/month.
- **Pilera**: From ~$0.10/unit/month (à la carte modules).

**Custom enterprise (targeting large management firms):**
- **CINC Systems**: ~$250/user/month estimated. Custom quotes only.
- **Vantaca**: Custom pricing. Enterprise-grade automation/AI.
- **Enumerate**: Custom pricing. 30+ year legacy platform.

**Key pricing insights:** No major HOA software offers a **genuine free tier** — a stark departure from SaaS norms. Transaction fees (credit card processing at 2.5–3.5% + per-transaction fees) add 30–60% to base costs. Annual billing discounts of 10–15% are standard. The $49/month price point has emerged as the de facto floor for small-HOA software.

The strategic whitespace sits below $49/month. For a 10-unit HOA with a $24,000 annual budget, even $49/month ($588/year) represents 2.5% of total revenue — material for a community run by cost-conscious volunteers. A **free or sub-$30 tier** would be unprecedented and could drive product-led growth.

## Unit economics framework for a new entrant

Building a viable HOA management SaaS business requires understanding the interplay between acquisition costs, lifetime value, and retention. Here is a model calibrated from SaaS benchmarks and HOA-specific data:

### Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

The most relevant benchmark comes from First Page Sage's proptech analysis: **$543 for SMB proptech customers**. Broader SaaS data supports a range:

| Channel | Estimated CAC |
|---|---|
| Organic SEO/content | $250–$500 |
| Referral programs | $100–$200 |
| Paid search (Google Ads) | $500–$900 |
| Software review sites (Capterra, G2) | $300–$600 |
| Trade shows/events | $800–$1,200 |
| **Blended target** | **$300–$600** |

A product-led growth motion (free tier → paid conversion) could push blended CAC below $200 at scale, as free-tier users convert organically.

### Lifetime Value (LTV) and churn

HOA software benefits from **moderate-to-high switching costs**: data migration is painful, board members resist change, and annual budget cycles create inertia. Industry-specific churn data is scarce, but proxy benchmarks suggest:

- **Monthly logo churn**: 2–4% (SMB SaaS norm is 3–5%; HOA switching costs push toward lower end)
- **Annual logo churn**: 15–30% for early-stage; **8–15%** once product-market fit is achieved
- **Annual revenue churn**: 10–20% early; **5–12%** at maturity

Using the mid-case scenario for an established product:

| Metric | Conservative | Mid-case | Optimistic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average monthly revenue per customer | $60 | $75 | $100 |
| Annual revenue per customer | $720 | $900 | $1,200 |
| Gross margin | 75% | 80% | 85% |
| Annual gross profit per customer | $540 | $720 | $1,020 |
| Average customer lifetime (years) | 3 | 5 | 7 |
| **Lifetime Value (LTV)** | **$1,620** | **$3,600** | **$7,140** |
| Blended CAC | $500 | $400 | $300 |
| **LTV:CAC ratio** | **3.2:1** | **9:1** | **23.8:1** |
| **CAC payback (months)** | **11** | **6.7** | **3.5** |

The **3:1 LTV:CAC minimum** for sustainable SaaS is achievable even in the conservative scenario. The mid-case, with a 5-year customer lifetime and $75/month ARPU, delivers excellent unit economics. Target **CAC payback under 12 months** — Bessemer Venture Partners recommends under 12 months for SMB SaaS, and the model achieves this across all scenarios.

### Expansion revenue potential

As communities grow or adopt additional features, **net revenue retention above 100%** becomes achievable. A community that starts at $49/month (50 units) might expand to $99/month as it grows or upgrades. Management companies that start with one community often manage dozens. Targeting **105–115% net revenue retention** is realistic for a well-designed product with expansion paths.

## Pricing strategy: lead with free, scale with value

For a new entrant targeting small self-managed HOAs with room to grow into larger communities and management companies, the optimal pricing architecture combines **product-led growth with tiered value capture**:

**Recommended pricing tiers:**

| Tier | Price | Units | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Free Forever** | $0/mo | 1–15 units | Unprecedented in HOA software. Covers basic dues tracking, communication, document storage. Drives organic adoption, reviews, and word-of-mouth. Converts 5–15% to paid. |
| **Starter** | $29/mo | 16–50 units | Below the $49 market floor. Adds online payment processing, violation tracking, basic reporting. Captures price-sensitive small HOAs. |
| **Standard** | $59/mo | 51–150 units | Full feature set including architectural reviews, advanced reporting, website builder, voting. Competitive with PayHOA's $99 tier but at lower price. |
| **Professional** | $99–$149/mo | 151–500 units | Adds multi-board support, advanced compliance tools, priority support. Positions for management company crossover. |
| **Enterprise** | Per-unit pricing | 500+ units or multi-community | Custom pricing at $0.30–$0.50/unit/month. Serves management companies managing multiple communities. |

**Critical pricing decisions:**
- **All features included at every paid tier** (following PayHOA's successful model). Differentiate on unit count, not feature gating — volunteer board members shouldn't have to evaluate complex feature matrices.
- **Transparent, published pricing.** The competitors that hide pricing (CINC, Vantaca, Enumerate) target enterprise. Self-managed HOA buyers demand transparency.
- **No setup fees, no contracts.** Month-to-month billing is table stakes. Offer 15% annual billing discount.
- **Payment processing fees**: Charge credit card fees at 2.99% + $0.30 (pass-through) and offer ACH/eCheck at $0.99. This creates meaningful ancillary revenue — a 100-unit HOA collecting $200/month in dues generates $2,400/year in payment volume, yielding ~$70–$100/year in processing margin.
- **Free onboarding and data migration.** This eliminates the biggest barrier to switching from spreadsheets and positions against competitors charging $99+ setup fees.

## Five channels to reach self-managed HOA board members

Self-managed HOA board members are volunteer decision-makers who research solutions primarily through Google search, peer recommendations, and review platforms. They are **not reached through enterprise sales motions**. Here are the five highest-impact channels, ranked by expected ROI:

**1. SEO and content marketing (highest long-term ROI).** Board members search for solutions when pain becomes acute — a treasurer inherits a mess of spreadsheets, dues are going uncollected, or the community faces a compliance issue. Target long-tail keywords like "self-managed HOA software for small community," "HOA dues collection app," and state-specific compliance terms ("Florida HOA annual report filing"). Build a content hub with state compliance guides, board member handbooks, budget templates, and transition guides. Competitors like PayHOA and HOALife already invest heavily here, but ultra-small HOA content (<25 units) is underserved.

**2. Software review platforms (fastest credibility signal).** Capterra lists 438 HOA software tools. G2, GetApp, and Software Advice are primary discovery channels. Getting listed with strong early reviews creates a compounding credibility advantage. Software Advice's advisory service directly connects buyers to vendors — vendors pay per qualified referral. Target placement in "best for small HOAs" and "most affordable" categories.

**3. Product-led growth via free tier (lowest CAC at scale).** A genuine free tier — unprecedented in HOA software — creates a viral loop. Board members who use the free product tell neighboring HOA boards. Reddit r/HOA and HOATalk.com (45,000+ members) become organic amplification channels as users recommend the product. Target **5–15% free-to-paid conversion** with in-product upgrade prompts triggered by unit count growth or feature needs.

**4. State HOA registries for direct outreach.** Texas maintains a searchable HOA database at hoa.texas.gov with names, counties, and contact information — available as an open dataset. Florida's Sunbiz.org and county property appraiser databases provide similar access. California, Arizona, Nevada, and Colorado all maintain searchable corporation registries where HOAs are filed as nonprofits. First American Data & Analytics offers a comprehensive nationwide commercial database. These enable targeted cold outreach via direct mail or email to HOA registered agents.

**5. Strategic partnerships and referral programs.** HOA attorneys, CPAs, and insurance agents interact regularly with self-managed boards and can serve as referral channels. The HOA-USA Partner Program provides vendor directory listings visited by "thousands of board members each month" across state-specific pages. CAI chapter events (not the expensive national conference) offer regional exposure — the NE Florida Expo offers free admission for board members, and the Illinois Condo-HOA Conference attracts 1,700+ attendees. Implement a referral program offering $50 credits for each community referred.

## Conclusion: a timing advantage in the largest SaaS whitespace you haven't heard of

The HOA management software market combines several characteristics that make it unusually attractive for a well-positioned new entrant. The TAM is **$1.5B–$2.5B and growing at 9–12% annually** — driven by structural forces (82% of new homes sold are in HOAs) rather than cyclical demand. The self-managed segment of ~110,000–130,000 communities represents a **$78M–$195M serviceable market** where 75% of potential customers use no dedicated software at all.

Unit economics are favorable: with a blended CAC of **$300–$600**, monthly ARPU of **$60–$100**, and customer lifetimes of **5+ years** driven by switching cost inertia, LTV:CAC ratios of **6:1–9:1** are achievable at scale. CAC payback periods under 12 months align with best-in-class SMB SaaS benchmarks.

The competitive landscape has a clear gap. No incumbent offers a **free tier**, and the market floor sits at $39–$49/month — expensive for a 10-unit community with a $24,000 annual budget. A product-led growth strategy anchored by a free forever tier, combined with transparent sub-$30 paid pricing, would create a wedge into the market's largest and most underserved segment. The key strategic bet: small self-managed HOAs that adopt your free product today become paying customers as they grow, and the board members who learn your tool carry that preference when they move to other communities or transition to professional management roles.
